An Introduction to Global Climate Shifts

Global warming, global climate change, global climate emergency. Whatever you call it, (nearly) everyone is aware that there have been shifts in the Earth's average temperatures.

But what does that really mean for the environment and our way of life?

In this visualization, we hope to demonstrate exactly how this
global increase in temperature relates to other climate events.

Surface Temperatures: On this page, we demonstrate that the earth's average surface temperature has indeed risen significantly since 1900. The original dataset assembled by Berkeley Earth can be found HERE.

Air & GHG Emissions: In here, we take a look at how each country is contributing to its effects. The original dataset from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) can be found HERE.

Surface Temperature & Emissions: Here, we demonstrate one of the main causes of this increase.

Wildfires in the United States: Finally, we look at how higher windspeeds due to topographical shifts can combine with this temperature increase to produce more wildfires, as we have seen in places like California and Australia in recent years. The original dataset from NASA can be found HERE.




Changes in Surface Temperature (1900 - 2013)

Changes in Surface
Temperatures Over Time:

This collection of maps shows how each country's average annual temperature (in Celsius) has deviated from its average over from 1900 to 2013. Increasingly dark shades of blue represent annual temperatures lower than the average, and red represents temperatures higher than the average.
If there were no global climate change, we would expect a fairly equal number of red and blue countries as we go through time -- fluctuation would be random. Note that as we get closer to 2013, we see much more red across the global and very little blue. This shows us that there is, in fact, a global temperature shift towards hotter temperatures.
Note also that not all countries have experienced the shift to the same degree. Countries closer to the poles in the northern hemisphere have experienced global temperature change at a much higher rate, which can be problematic since much of the earthâ€™s freshwater is stored in these countries within ice packs. However, just because these countries experience temperature change to a more extreme degree, does not mean they are the only ones affected. Countries in more moderate or even tropical regions can face challenges in the face of global temperature change, as some climates are extremely sensitive to even slight shifts in temperature. Desertification, drought, and flooding are all things that can contribute to devastating outcomes such as crop failure in some of the most food-insecure regions of the world.
